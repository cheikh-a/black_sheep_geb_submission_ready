Please consider the manuscript *No Country for Honest Men: Balanced Theft, Endogenous Patronage, and the Emergence of Enforcement* for publication in *Games and Economic Behavior*.

The paper studies a predatory order in which reciprocal theft can sustain equality so long as participation remains universal, then asks how that order changes once one household becomes permanently honest. The analytical core begins from a unit transfer benchmark, characterizes balanced theft cycles as weak Nash equilibria under symmetry, and shows in an infinite-horizon repeated environment with stationary Markov behavior that a single honest rupture destroys stationarity of the original cycle once exposure becomes wealth dependent. A proportional transfer extension then endogenizes class differentiation, delegated predation, and the later turn from hiring thieves to hiring guards.

The paper is written for *Games and Economic Behavior* because the contribution is game theoretic at each stage of the argument. The equilibrium object is stated explicitly, the contracting problem that generates patron surplus is derived rather than asserted, and the move toward guarding arises from a comparison of control technologies inside the same strategic environment. The simulation component does not replace the theory. It takes the same primitives and payoff comparisons to a richer state space in order to map regime transitions, comparative statics, inequality dynamics, and welfare consequences that are difficult to characterize in closed form.

The manuscript begins from Italo Calvino's "The Black Sheep," but the literary source is used to motivate a formal problem rather than to substitute for one. The paper connects corruption equilibria, convention selection, appropriation, hierarchy, and endogenous enforcement within a single sequence from reciprocal predation to selective protection. That sequence, together with the distinction between analytical and computational results, appears to fit the broad game theory and institutions audience of the journal.

Code, simulation outputs, and the full figure-building workflow are archived on Zenodo at [10.5281/zenodo.19051109](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19051109).
