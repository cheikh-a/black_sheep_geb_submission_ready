\begin{abstract}
This paper studies how a reciprocally predatory order unravels once one household withdraws from it, taking its motivating problem from \emph{The Black Sheep}. The benchmark is a repeated raiding game in which each household leaves home, each unoccupied house is targeted once, and a balanced theft cycle survives as a weak Nash equilibrium of the stage game. Under stationary Markov behavior, one permanently honest household remains home, blocks the incoming raid, and shifts wealth on the first night from its predecessor to its successor; when leaving home exposes current wealth, that windfall moves the successor above the cutoff at which personal raiding remains privately attractive, so the original cycle cannot persist as a stationary continuation. The analysis then extends the game to proportional loot, delegated predation, and guarding. Rich households hire thieves when delegation creates surplus over the worker's outside option and contracting cost, and they switch to guards when concentrated wealth and prey depletion raise the value of protection above the value of continued patronage. Simulations built on the same occupation, targeting, and success rules trace the transition from reciprocal theft to patronage and selective protection and show how that transition raises inequality and lowers welfare once theft, contracting, guarding, and punishment absorb resources.
\end{abstract}

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} repeated games, corruption equilibria, patronage, endogenous enforcement, inequality
