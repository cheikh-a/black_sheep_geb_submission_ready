\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The opening order remains stable only in the narrow accounting sense captured by the unit transfer benchmark. Each household loses once and gains once, so equality survives even while predation is universal. Lemma \ref{lem:first-night} and Theorem \ref{thm:no-stationary-one-honest} then show how little is required to destroy that balance. One permanently honest household blocks one incoming raid, leaves one successor unraided, and creates a local wealth difference large enough to matter once personal raiding also exposes current wealth. The later environments amplify that initial asymmetry through new margins. Proposition \ref{prop:patronage} requires a surplus from delegation over the worker outside option. Theorem \ref{thm:guarding} requires concentrated wealth and effective protection to make guarding privately preferable to one more round of delegated predation. Proposition \ref{prop:welfare} turns the same sequence into a welfare statement by combining concavity in wealth with the resource costs of theft, contracting, guarding, and punishment. The figures then supply timing, magnitude, and regime prevalence for those margins once many heterogeneous agents interact.

No full Markov perfect equilibrium is solved for the stochastic environment with matching, delegation, and guarding. Production is absent, so the model studies redistribution, dissipation, and protection of wealth rather than the emergence of a productive property-rights order. The final regime accordingly remains a protected unequal order built on stocks accumulated during the transition itself. The simulations make visible the state dependence that lies beyond the closed-form results. Event-time figures isolate the first-night perturbation and the resulting exposure threshold. Contracting figures identify the surplus region in which delegation is feasible. The phase diagram and robustness panels show where the guarding threshold is reached, where patronage remains transitional, and where depletion collapses the mechanism before a stable protected elite can consolidate. A broader coalition of honest households could generate a different path, and a model with production or a richer public-goods technology could generate a different endpoint.

Calvino's fable remains useful because it compresses the whole movement into one thought experiment: universal predation can look balanced from within, a local honest rupture can destroy that balance, and the order that follows can be quieter without becoming more equal. Each step now rests on an explicit game, a restricted equilibrium class, and a small set of threshold results. The simulations fill in the transition path that those results leave open. Reciprocity, delegation, and protection are linked throughout by stated actions, stated payoffs, and stated conditions.
