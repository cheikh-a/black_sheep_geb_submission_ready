\section{Introduction}

In \emph{The Black Sheep}, Calvino begins with a society in which nightly theft is universal and equality survives only because every household is both predator and prey. Each loss is offset by another gain, so wealth remains level while the society lives under generalized predation. The story becomes economically sharp when one man stays at home. His abstention removes one link from the circuit, leaves the burglar who targeted him empty handed, and gives a windfall to the household that would otherwise have been robbed by that burglar. From there the story moves toward hierarchy: some households become rich, poor households steal on behalf of the rich, and guards and police later protect fortunes accumulated during the predatory phase \citep{calvino_1993}. The sequence is literary in origin and economically precise in structure. The opening balance can be formalized as a regime of reciprocal predation that reproduces itself only under exact accounting. A society of thieves remains equal only when each household occupies the same position in the transfer network, so the first question is how local abstention changes continuation incentives inside that network. The later stages pose a different problem because decentralized theft, delegated theft, and organized protection do not rest on the same primitives. An environment rich enough to generate class formation obscures the accounting that sustains the initial cycle, while the benchmark that isolates the cycle cannot on its own generate hierarchy or enforcement. Reciprocal balance is easiest to see in a unit transfer benchmark in which every successful raid moves one unit, every household begins with the same wealth, and a balanced cycle is a permutation in which every agent has one outgoing raid and one incoming raid. In that environment wealth is preserved period by period, and any such cycle is a weak Nash equilibrium under symmetry. The benchmark remains intentionally narrow because it does not yet contain the ingredients needed for hierarchy or enforcement; its role is to isolate the exact form of reciprocity that Calvino places at the start of the story and to give precise content to the claim that universal participation can sustain equality even inside a predatory order.

The honest perturbation is studied in an infinite horizon repeated version of the benchmark, where the relevant equilibrium object is a stationary Markov regime rather than a one period game or the full set of repeated outcomes supported by elaborate punishments. One agent is permanently honest and remains at home every period. All other agents choose whether to steal personally or remain at home as a function of current wealth. The first honest night creates a local windfall because one intended victim is no longer robbed, while the predecessor who targeted the honest house fails to obtain loot. Once leaving home exposes wealth left at home, that windfall changes at least one adjacent continuation payoff. Under a transparent wealth threshold condition, the original pattern of balanced reciprocity cannot remain stationary with exactly one permanently honest agent. A second environment is needed for the later stages, because a society organized around universal corruption does not move from corruption to honesty in one step but first toward stratified predation and only later toward selective protection. Making that transition economically coherent requires an explanation of why richer households stop stealing personally, why poorer households accept predatory work on behalf of the rich, and why those same elites later prefer guards to thieves. The proportional transfer extension supplies those margins. When theft scales with victim wealth, a rich household that goes out to steal exposes a larger stock at home. Delegation can then dominate personal theft if delegated predation has a targeting advantage and poor workers face weak outside options. Later, once elite wealth becomes large and the prey base among the poor is depleted, the value of protection overtakes the value of one more round of delegated predation. The chronology then runs from reciprocal theft to patronage and from patronage to guarding through explicit payoff comparisons.

Several literatures bear directly on that sequence. Corruption equilibria remain privately coherent when individual choices change the environment faced by others \citep{andvig_moene_1990,bardhan_1997,shleifer_vishny_1993,tirole_1996}. Conventions and equilibrium selection explain how local perturbations move societies across sustained orders \citep{young_1993}. Appropriation and protection allocate effort between predation and defense under insecure claims \citep{skaperdas_1992,grossman_kim_1995,hirshleifer_1995}. Hierarchy and delegated organization explain how vertical control redistributes rents \citep{olsen_torsvik_1998}. Endogenous institutions explain why organized enforcement often protects concentrated advantage \citep{olson_1993,north_wallis_weingast_2009,besley_persson_2009,acemoglu_ticchi_vindigni_2011}. The contribution here is to place those margins inside one transition from balanced reciprocal theft to patronage and then to selective protection. The analogy to top trading cycles needs careful handling because it is useful and easy to overstate. A balanced theft cycle resembles a cyclic allocation graph in one limited respect: each agent points outward and is pointed to by exactly one other agent. That graph structure helps explain why the benchmark preserves equality under symmetry and why a local break has consequences beyond the abstainer himself. But the analogy stops at the graph. In the Shapley and Scarf environment, transfers are voluntary and stability is defined through blocking coalitions \citep{shapley_scarf_1974}. In the present environment, theft is coercive, generates externalities, and changes later opportunities through exposure and depletion. The cyclic analogy therefore serves only as a disciplined description of structure. Claims about the core or about mechanism design do not survive the change in environment.

Because the benchmark conserves aggregate wealth by construction, aggregate wealth alone cannot distinguish regimes. Period welfare is therefore defined as the sum of concave utilities over wealth minus theft effort costs, contracting costs, guarding costs, and expected punishment losses. This object separates margins that would otherwise be mixed together: pure transfers need not change total wealth, the same transfers can lower utilitarian welfare once they produce inequality, and the later movement from reciprocal theft to patronage and guarding can lower welfare further because predation and protection consume resources. The analytical results leave open questions of timing, prevalence, regime duration, and comparative statics that are difficult to settle in closed form once many heterogeneous agents interact. The same payoff comparisons derived in the theory are therefore embedded in an explicit transition process and studied in simulation. The resulting paths reproduce the first honest perturbation in event time, trace class formation in the proportional environment, map parameter regions associated with patronage, coexistence, guarding, and collapse, and quantify the evolution of inequality and welfare along the transition.

Two restrictions structure the analysis from the outset. No full Markov perfect equilibrium is solved for the large stochastic state space generated by delegated theft, matching frictions, and protection choices,\footnote{The theory identifies the margins and thresholds that matter. The simulation studies how those margins interact once the full state space becomes too large for complete analytical characterization.} and production is left outside the model. Wealth is redistributed, dissipated through cost, and protected without being endogenously created, which isolates the political economy of predation but also means that the final guard regime should be read as protection of accumulated spoils rather than as a complete productive property-rights order.
