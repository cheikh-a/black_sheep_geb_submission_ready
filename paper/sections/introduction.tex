\section{Introduction}

This paper studies how a reciprocally predatory order unravels when one household withdraws from it. In \emph{The Black Sheep}, Calvino begins from a society in which theft is universal and equality survives because every household is both predator and prey, so each loss is offset by a matching gain and predation reproduces itself without generating stratification \citep{calvino_1993}. Once one man stays home, that accounting breaks locally and then keeps unfolding through hierarchy, delegation, and policing. The opening order can be written as a unit transfer raiding game in which each household chooses in every period whether to remain home or which other house to raid, occupied houses are safe in the benchmark, and concurrent raiders divide one rival loot. Under a balanced theft cycle every household leaves home, every unoccupied house is targeted once, and wealth remains level because each household has one incoming loss and one outgoing gain. Universal predation is therefore compatible with equality through exact accounting, and the opening order survives as a weak Nash equilibrium of the stage game.

The repeated version keeps the same benchmark game, imposes an infinite horizon, and restricts behavior to stationary Markov strategies. One household is permanently honest and therefore remains at home in every period. If the remaining households follow the old cycle on the first perturbed date, the predecessor who targets the honest house returns empty handed, while the successor escapes the raid that would otherwise have hit him and keeps the proceeds of his own theft. Once leaving home also exposes current wealth, that windfall can move the successor above the cutoff at which personal raiding ceases to be privately attractive, so the original balanced cycle cannot survive as a stationary continuation. From that point onward, exact unit transfers are too narrow to carry the rest of the argument, because hierarchy becomes meaningful only after wealth differences begin to matter for choice. The extension therefore preserves the same population, the same public wealth state, and the same logic of explicit target choice while replacing unit loot with proportional loot and enlarging the occupational menu. Rich households compare personal raiding, staying home, hiring thieves, and later hiring guards; poor households compare freelance theft, paid predation, protection work, and withdrawal. Patronage requires delegated predation to reach targets more effectively than freelancers can and poor workers to face outside options weak enough to leave a surplus after wages and contracting cost. Guarding appears later, once concentrated wealth and prey depletion alter the ranking of control technologies. Hierarchy, delegation, and selective protection are thus tied to explicit payoff comparisons inside the same extended environment rather than appended as loose stages of a narrative.

That sequence sits at the intersection of several literatures without fitting comfortably inside any one of them. Corruption equilibria remain privately coherent because each participant takes as given an order sustained by the participation of others \citep{andvig_moene_1990,bardhan_1997,shleifer_vishny_1993,tirole_1996}. Local perturbations then matter through the same logic that convention and equilibrium-selection models use to study movement across sustained orders \citep{young_1993}. Appropriation and defense become central once predation and protection compete for resources under insecure claims \citep{skaperdas_1992,grossman_kim_1995,hirshleifer_1995}, while hierarchy enters once the rich redirect predatory labor through vertical organization \citep{olsen_torsvik_1998}. Organized enforcement appears only after the transition has already created an unequal order worth defending \citep{olson_1993,north_wallis_weingast_2009,besley_persson_2009,acemoglu_ticchi_vindigni_2011}. The graph of the balanced benchmark also recalls a directed exchange cycle, which is why \citet{shapley_scarf_1974} remains useful as a limited structural reference, although the connection ends there because transfers here are coercive, occupancy changes the opportunity set of others, and rivalry in extraction creates externalities absent from voluntary exchange.

Aggregate wealth cannot be the welfare object because the benchmark preserves it by construction. Period welfare is therefore defined as the sum of concave utilities over wealth minus theft effort costs, contracting costs, guarding costs, and expected punishment losses. That criterion separates pure transfer from inequality and resource dissipation, and it also fixes the role of the simulations.\footnote{The theory isolates the margins that can be derived cleanly. The simulation section studies how those margins interact once the extended state space becomes too large for complete analytical characterization.} No full Markov perfect equilibrium is solved for the large stochastic state space generated by matching, delegated predation, and protection, and production remains outside the model. The simulations instead implement the same action logic, targeting rules, and threshold comparisons used in the theory, then measure transition timing, regime prevalence, inequality, and welfare along paths that the analytical results do not characterize in closed form.
