\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:literature}

The setting in Calvino belongs to a familiar concern in economics, namely the possibility that conduct which is plainly undesirable in welfare terms can nevertheless form a coherent and durable equilibrium. In the corruption literature this concern appears in several adjacent forms. \citet{andvig_moene_1990} show how corruption reproduces itself by altering the incentives faced by later actors. \citet{bardhan_1997} emphasizes the institutional complementarities that make corrupt systems persistent. \citet{shleifer_vishny_1993} show that the social effects of corruption depend on whether predation is fragmented or coordinated, while \citet{tirole_1996} studies how collective reputations trap organizations in persistent patterns of misconduct even when many participants would prefer a cleaner order. The same concern appears here in a narrower and more sequential setting. The object is a world of reciprocal thieves and the transition set off by a small honest rupture that disturbs the accounting on which reciprocity depended.

The same disturbance also places the analysis near the literature on conventions and equilibrium selection. \citet{young_1993} studies how recurrent interaction and local perturbations move a society from one sustained convention to another. The central insight is that a local change can shift the continuation incentives of neighboring actors and thereby alter the basin of attraction of the entire system. The opening equilibrium in Calvino has exactly that structure. Its persistence is behavioral rather than normative. Under symmetry it is regular and reproducible even though it rests on universal predation. The first honest agent interrupts a pattern of mutual dependence, and the first windfall together with the first shortfall changes who still finds participation privately worthwhile.

The movement from reciprocal predation to hierarchical predation requires a different body of work. Much of the corruption literature begins with an organization that is already stratified and then studies collusion, monitoring, or internal incentive design. \citet{olsen_torsvik_1998} are especially useful because hierarchy there alters both the distribution of rents and the feasibility of corruption itself. The same problem arises once rich households stop stealing personally and begin to buy predatory labor from poor households. The difference is that hierarchy is not assumed at the start. It has to be generated from the breakdown of a decentralized arrangement. Patronage therefore requires a strict surplus condition. Delegation cannot rest on vague appeals to elite power alone. It must be profitable because a patron directs theft more effectively than a freelancer can and because the reservation utility of poor workers remains compressed.

\citet{skaperdas_1992} and \citet{grossman_kim_1995} study environments in which insecure claims induce agents to allocate resources between conflict and defense. \citet{hirshleifer_1995} sharpens the same theme by analyzing the conditions under which anarchy unravels and more selective forms of order emerge. \citet{demsetz_1967} remains a useful background reference because property rights there arise when internalization becomes valuable enough to justify protection. The connection is close in its treatment of appropriation and protection as alternative uses of resources, but the point of departure matters. The starting point is an order in which insecurity of claims is universal, reciprocal, and roughly egalitarian. The later rise of guarding is therefore not the first appearance of order. One order replaces another, and the later order protects a distribution created during the transition itself.

The connection to state formation and endogenous enforcement is equally direct. \citet{olson_1993} distinguishes roving bandits from stationary ones and argues that an encompassing interest in future extraction can create incentives to provide order. \citet{north_wallis_weingast_2009} generalize that intuition into a theory of violence orders organized around elite management of rents. \citet{besley_persson_2009} study the conditions under which legal and fiscal capacity emerge, while \citet{acemoglu_ticchi_vindigni_2011} show that inefficient coercive states can persist once they serve the interests of those who control them. \citet{acemoglu_robinson_2008} likewise stress that institutional change often preserves elite power. The final regime belongs in that lineage. Guards and police arise because concentrated wealth becomes worth defending and because labor can be reallocated from predation to protection without changing who controls the terms of social order.

Once guarding enters the picture, \citet{becker_1968} and \citet{becker_stigler_1974} also become relevant because elite households no longer face only the choice between stealing and abstaining. No benevolent state chooses optimal sanctions here, and no optimal policing problem is solved, but the comparison does shift toward two costly technologies, one extractive and one protective, each with its own labor demand and effect on future incentives.

Directed cycles matter here only as a graph theoretic analogy. A balanced theft cycle is also a directed cycle with one incoming and one outgoing link for each agent, and in \citet{shapley_scarf_1974} directed cycles organize voluntary exchange among agents who reassign indivisible objects. The analogy is useful at that level of structure and no further. The transfers here are coercive, alter later incentives through exposure and depletion, and impose externalities on actors who have not consented to any reassignment, so claims about the core, strategy proofness, or voluntary stability do not survive the change in environment.

\citet{skaperdas_1992} and \citet{grossman_kim_1995} keep attention on appropriation and defense under insecure claims. \citet{becker_1968} and \citet{becker_stigler_1974} keep attention on sanction, precaution, and the incentives of enforcement. \citet{olson_1993}, \citet{north_wallis_weingast_2009}, \citet{besley_persson_2009}, and \citet{acemoglu_ticchi_vindigni_2011} show how organized coercion stabilizes extraction and protects concentrated advantage. Their intersection is the natural place for a setting that begins from egalitarian reciprocal corruption, because aggregate wealth cannot serve as the relevant welfare object once the benchmark preserves total wealth by construction. The comparison has to run through concavity in wealth together with the costs of theft, punishment, contracting, and guarding. Reciprocal corruption, hierarchical predation, and selective protection then appear as successive forms of one order, linked by a local honest rupture that shifts incentives without generating an impartial institutional exit.
