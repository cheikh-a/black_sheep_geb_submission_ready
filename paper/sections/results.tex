\section{Simulation Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{The first honest night}

Balanced reciprocity first breaks through an exact accounting, which is why Figures \ref{fig:fig01_balanced_cycle_event} and \ref{fig:fig02_first_night_accounting} precede any discussion of long horizon inequality or institutional emergence. Figure \ref{fig:fig01_balanced_cycle_event} follows the honest agent, the predecessor, the successor, and the remaining population in event time. The structure is exactly the one derived in Lemma \ref{lem:first-night}. The honest agent does not suffer an immediate loss because he is at home on the first night. The predecessor who attempts to rob that occupied house receives no loot and is still robbed by his own predecessor, so his wealth falls. The successor, who would otherwise have been robbed by the honest agent, keeps the proceeds of his own raid and receives the first windfall. The first break therefore takes the form of a precise redistribution over a three agent neighborhood of the original cycle.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig01_balanced_cycle_event.pdf}
    \caption{Balanced reciprocity breaks on the first honest night. The successor of the honest agent receives an immediate windfall, while the predecessor who attempts to rob the honest house returns empty handed. The figure demonstrates the local asymmetry that starts the transition.}
    \label{fig:fig01_balanced_cycle_event}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:fig02_first_night_accounting} compresses the same event into a cleaner accounting display. The honest agent is protected on the first night. The first winner is the successor, not the abstainer. The first loser is the predecessor who raids an occupied house and returns with nothing. That chronology matters because the stationary instability theorem rests on the successor windfall. The state change pushes at least one neighboring agent above the exposure threshold. Immediate victimization of the honest agent would produce a different theorem and a different simulation path.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mediumfigwidth]{figures/fig02_first_night_accounting.pdf}
    \caption{First night accounting. The honest agent is protected on the first night and is robbed only later in the transition; the winners and losers on that night are therefore the successor and predecessor in the original cycle. The figure demonstrates the exact timeline that underlies the instability theorem.}
    \label{fig:fig02_first_night_accounting}
\end{figure}

Because the target structure contains no noise, the departure from the original cycle can be attributed to equilibrium logic instead of chance. Once the successor wealth exceeds the threshold in \eqref{eq:threshold}, the continuation path begins to involve further choices to stay home and new interruptions in the network. That is exactly the mechanism stated in Theorem \ref{thm:no-stationary-one-honest}, and the event time display allows the reader to watch the theorem unfold in visible form.

\subsection{From local asymmetry to class formation}

The longer horizon simulations show that the first honest shock does not merely reshuffle labels on who is temporarily ahead and who is behind. It generates persistent class structure. Figure \ref{fig:fig03_event_time_roles} groups agents by eventual role in the proportional transfer environment and then traces the resulting event time wealth paths. The separation occurs early. Agents who later become patrons begin to pull away almost immediately after the initial disturbance. Hired thieves remain below them, guards emerge later from the lower tail, and the residual poor stay clustered near the bottom. The timing matters as much as the spread. Differentiation begins before patronage becomes the dominant regime. That is consistent with the theory. Rich households emerge first because personal exposure changes the occupational choice of early windfall recipients. Patronage enters after that distribution has already begun to separate.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig03_event_time_roles.pdf}
    \caption{Event time wealth trajectories by eventual role. Agents who later become patrons separate quickly from hired thieves, guards, and the residual poor. The figure demonstrates that class differentiation appears early in the transition and is not only an artifact of the terminal date.}
    \label{fig:fig03_event_time_roles}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:fig04_distribution_evolution} shows the same process in cross section. The heatmap and quantile ribbons reveal a distribution that rapidly loses its middle. In early periods the support remains narrow because the honest perturbation is still local. A few periods later the upper tail thickens while the lower quantiles bend downward as repeated raids and weak outside options trap a growing mass of agents in the poor set. The widening is not symmetric. The upper tail is carried by a small number of agents who stop exposing their own wealth and later capture part of delegated loot, while the lower tail contains agents who continue to circulate through risky occupations and increasingly depleted target pools. The simulation is therefore producing more than heterogeneity. It is producing a different allocation of risk, power, and occupational choice across the distribution.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig04_distribution_evolution.pdf}
    \caption{Evolution of the wealth distribution. The heatmap and quantile bands show the rapid thinning of the middle and the thickening of both tails. The figure demonstrates that the honest perturbation produces durable stratification instead of a temporary reshuffle.}
    \label{fig:fig04_distribution_evolution}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:fig05_inequality_dynamics} translates that cross sectional movement into conventional distributional statistics. The Gini coefficient rises sharply from its benchmark value near zero. The top decile wealth share increases in tandem. Lorenz curves at selected dates move steadily away from the forty five degree line. The summary statistics matter because they show that the transition is not confined to a few pathwise anecdotes. The distributional consequences are large, systematic, and persistent in the baseline calibration.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig05_inequality_dynamics.pdf}
    \caption{Inequality dynamics. The Gini coefficient, top decile wealth share, and Lorenz curves all move sharply away from the egalitarian benchmark. The figure demonstrates that the transition has strong and persistent distributional consequences.}
    \label{fig:fig05_inequality_dynamics}
\end{figure}

What these displays rule out is a simple narrative in which the move from honesty to inequality is merely literary convenience. In the model, the deterministic perturbation creates a state difference, the exposure threshold converts that difference into differentiated behavior, and the proportional environment amplifies the behavioral difference because the rich internalize exposure while the poor remain available for predatory labor. The computational output supports the theoretical sequence and keeps it from collapsing into a black box path.

\subsection{Patronage and the surplus from delegation}

Across an interpretable range of parameters, rich agents hire poor thieves only when the contract creates surplus that is both feasible and privately valuable. Figure \ref{fig:fig06_contracting_comparative_statics} examines that condition directly. The left panel varies the targeting advantage of delegation. The right panel traces the gap between compensation and the outside option as worker slackness changes. Patron surplus rises with the first margin and falls as the second margin strengthens, exactly as Proposition \ref{prop:patronage} predicts. Delegated theft that is better targeted than freelancing leaves room to satisfy participation and still retain a positive residual. A stronger outside option narrows that residual and can eliminate it.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig06_contracting_comparative_statics.pdf}
    \caption{Contracting comparative statics. Patron surplus rises when delegated theft has a stronger targeting advantage and poor outside options are weaker. The figure demonstrates the explicit source of patron surplus and removes the earlier zero profit knife edge.}
    \label{fig:fig06_contracting_comparative_statics}
\end{figure}

Delegation becomes profitable only because the environment generates a clear wedge between delegated returns and the outside option of the thief. If the thief could always obtain alone the same gross loot he could obtain for the patron, and if the patron had neither targeting advantage nor bargaining power, patronage would collapse to a zero-profit transfer. Here the patron directs predation more effectively or protects the contract well enough to make delegation more productive than freelancing, while the thief operates in a crowded and depleted market. The gap between delegated returns and the outside option pays the contracting cost and still leaves a residual for the patron.

Patronage need not be monotone in every parameter that raises expected loot. When theft intensity becomes too large, the prey base can be depleted quickly, which shortens the durability of the contract regime even when delegation is initially attractive. Proposition \ref{prop:patronage} gives the static dominance condition; the simulations trace its interaction with depletion, competition, and regime duration.

\subsection{From patronage to guarding}

Figure \ref{fig:fig07_regime_phase_diagram} maps the dominant medium run regime over the parameter plane spanned by theft intensity and enforcement effectiveness. Several patterns are worth emphasizing. Low theft intensity combined with weak enforcement tends to preserve a long transition because the returns to both predation and protection remain limited. As theft intensity rises and enforcement becomes more effective, the dominant regime shifts toward coexistence and guarding. Collapse appears where predation is intense enough and protection weak enough that the social base is depleted before a stable protected elite can consolidate. This is the computational analogue of Theorem \ref{thm:guarding}. Once concentrated wealth becomes valuable enough to protect and protection becomes sufficiently effective, elites prefer to preserve their claims and stop financing one more round of delegated extraction.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mediumfigwidth]{figures/fig07_regime_phase_diagram.pdf}
    \caption{Regime phase diagram. Different combinations of theft intensity and enforcement effectiveness lead to distinct medium run transition, coexistence, guard, and collapse regions. The figure demonstrates that the successor regimes of the model occupy interpretable parts of the parameter space and do not arise from a single baseline path.}
    \label{fig:fig07_regime_phase_diagram}
\end{figure}

On this particular parameter plane patronage appears primarily as a transitional corridor and only infrequently as the dominant medium run regime. The diagram is therefore populated mainly by transition, coexistence, guard, and collapse regions. Patronage remains visible in the event time paths, the network snapshots, and the contracting comparative statics. The phase diagram adds the further point that patronage need not survive as the dominant institutional form once enforcement becomes effective enough or depletion becomes severe enough.

Figure \ref{fig:fig08_role_shares} restores time to the same regime logic. The share of agents engaged in direct or freelance theft falls after the first perturbation. Patrons and hired thieves rise next. Guards appear later and remain active through much of the enforcement phase, even though their share is not monotone over time. The plot works as an occupational decomposition of the whole mechanism. The early decline in universal theft reflects the exposure logic of Theorem \ref{thm:no-stationary-one-honest}. The rise in patron and hired thief roles reflects the surplus logic of Proposition \ref{prop:patronage}. The later appearance of guarding reflects the threshold logic of Theorem \ref{thm:guarding}.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig08_role_shares.pdf}
    \caption{Role shares over time. Universal theft gives way first to patronage and later to selective protection, with guarding remaining present through much of the enforcement phase. The figure demonstrates the endogenous reorganization of the social hierarchy after the first honest shock.}
    \label{fig:fig08_role_shares}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:fig09_transition_timing} separates the part of the transition that is pinned by chronology from the part that is not. Under the simulation timing, the first opportunity to hire arrives immediately after the deterministic honest shock, and every run generates at least one patron at that first post-shock contracting round. A raw first-entry statistic for patronage is therefore uninformative. The left panel instead measures the scale of the patronage complex at time $3$, after the first hiring cascade has unfolded. The right panel turns to the later margin that the chronology does not pin down mechanically and records the date at which guards reach five percent of the population. Broader honest shocks do not produce a smooth one-for-one acceleration in this clustered-shock sweep, because enlarging the initial honest set changes the local geometry of the broken cycle as well as its size. What the figure does show clearly is immediate reorganization into patronage and a later, more dispersed transition into enforcement once wealth concentration and prey depletion have progressed far enough.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig09_transition_timing.pdf}
    \caption{Early patronage and later guard timing. The first patron appears at the earliest feasible post-shock contracting round in every run, so the left panel reports the share of patrons and hired thieves at time $3$. The right panel reports the time at which guards reach five percent of the population. The figure demonstrates immediate organizational change after the honest shock and a later, more dispersed movement into enforcement.}
    \label{fig:fig09_transition_timing}
\end{figure}

Coexistence reveals institutional layering and rules out a synchronized regime switch. In those paths, some elites have crossed the guard threshold while others still rely on patronage. The resulting society is neither one of decentralized theft nor one of uniform protection. It is a mixed order in which different technologies of control are used at the same time by households in different parts of the wealth distribution. That layering matters substantively. The move from patronage to guarding is itself unequal and sequential. Some elites buy security before others can, which stabilizes their lead.

It summarizes successive layers of one model after the timing, regime, and occupational evidence has been laid out. The baseline perturbation remains close to the deterministic benchmark and therefore produces only modest inequality. Patronage produces a larger rise in concentration because it adds a contract technology that channels loot upward. Enforcement stabilizes that concentration. In that sense the table mirrors Proposition \ref{prop:welfare}. Distributional divergence is already visible once reciprocity breaks. Later regimes magnify that divergence or lock it in through new organizational choices.

\subsection{Network reorganization and welfare}

Figure \ref{fig:fig10_network_snapshots} makes the organizational change visible in network form. On the first honest night the graph is still dominated by direct theft edges arranged around the original cycle. In the middle snapshot, contract links connect patrons to hired thieves and theft edges point from those thieves toward vulnerable poor households. In the later snapshot, guard links radiate from elite households while direct theft edges thin out. More than wealth is being redistributed. The pattern of social linkage itself changes. Horizontal predation gives way to vertical contracting, which later gives way to selective protection.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig10_network_snapshots.pdf}
    \caption{Network snapshots across regimes. Direct theft edges dominate at first, contract edges later connect patrons to thieves, and guard edges eventually organize elite protection. The figure demonstrates the changing network structure of predation and control.}
    \label{fig:fig10_network_snapshots}
\end{figure}

The welfare consequences appear in Figure \ref{fig:fig11_welfare_decomposition}. Gross wealth and utilitarian welfare do not move together. Aggregate wealth declines steadily because theft, punishment, and protection burn resources even when much of the interaction is redistributive in form. Welfare deteriorates more sharply because redistribution toward the top lowers the concave utility sum and because predation, contracting, punishment, and guarding all consume resources. This is the logic of Proposition \ref{prop:welfare}. Low welfare in the transition does not arise from honesty itself. It arises from the unequal and resource-consuming adjustment that follows an incomplete honest shock.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig11_welfare_decomposition.pdf}
    \caption{Welfare decomposition. Aggregate wealth and utilitarian welfare diverge because the transition adds inequality and resource burning activity in the form of theft, contracts, punishment, and guarding. The figure demonstrates the welfare mechanism directly.}
    \label{fig:fig11_welfare_decomposition}
\end{figure}

The figure follows one baseline enforcement path rather than a comparison across parameter regions, so its evidentiary role is narrower than the corollary. What it shows directly is a shift in the composition of resource use. Theft costs dominate early, contracting costs spike during patronage, and guarding costs become persistent later on. Guard adoption therefore changes how resources are burned without restoring the earlier welfare level. The protected order that emerges at the end still rests on concentrated wealth accumulated during the predatory transition.

\subsection{Robustness}

Figure \ref{fig:fig12_robustness_panels} collects the main one-dimensional robustness exercises in compact form. Final welfare is most responsive to theft intensity and occupied-house vulnerability in this sweep, while final inequality moves within a narrower band. The point markers also show how little regime classification moves once the system is already in the region where guarding is attractive. Among the four margins shown, only the theft-intensity sweep changes the dominant regime, passing from transition to guard and then to coexistence as extraction becomes harsher. The other sweeps mainly alter the severity of the outcome rather than the identity of the final regime. These panels therefore support a restrained robustness claim: the mechanism does not depend on a single hand-picked calibration, but some margins move welfare far more than they move regime labels.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\mainfigwidth]{figures/fig12_robustness_panels.pdf}
    \caption{Robustness panels. The top row reports final inequality, the bottom row reports final welfare, and point markers identify the dominant regime reached at each parameter value. The figure demonstrates that the mechanism does not depend on a single hand-picked calibration, while also showing that some margins move welfare more than regime labels.}
    \label{fig:fig12_robustness_panels}
\end{figure}

These failure cases are informative because the mechanism operates only under a distinct and interpretable set of conditions. High contracting costs and weak targeting advantage compress the delegation surplus, while low occupied-house vulnerability reduces the value of moving from presence alone to organized protection. The sweeps therefore identify which margins attenuate the transition without pretending that every one-dimensional variation redraws the institutional map.

Figure \ref{fig:fig07_regime_phase_diagram} remains the more informative display for collapse. There the collapse cells are neither the moral opposite of balanced reciprocity nor a sign of numerical instability. They identify paths in which predation and protection together destroy the social base that made them profitable. Poor households become too depleted to sustain patronage, and inequality becomes so sharp that a small protected core cannot reorganize the rest into a stable order. This possibility matters because it prevents the model from telling a falsely deterministic story in which every corrupt equilibrium matures smoothly into guarded hierarchy.
