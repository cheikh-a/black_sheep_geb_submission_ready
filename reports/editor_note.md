# Editor Note

This revision narrows the paper's contribution and strengthens it.

Analytically, the paper now does four things cleanly: it defines a balanced unit-transfer theft benchmark; it states a repeated-game instability result for a permanent honest perturbation; it derives the condition under which rich agents prefer delegated theft to personal theft or abstention; and it derives the condition under which those same agents switch from thieves to guards.

Computationally, the paper uses a reproducible R pipeline to map the transition path and the regime space. The simulations are used for timing, comparative statics, and welfare magnitudes, not for claims that the theory section does not support.

The remaining limitations are explicit. The paper does not solve the full Markov-perfect equilibrium of the large stochastic state space, and it does not include production. The contribution is instead a disciplined theory-plus-simulation account of how a reciprocal corruption regime can break into patronage and then enforcement after a small honest shock.
